Money is measured in grains and ounces, not paper and pixels.

The United States Dollar once had a standard mass. The Coinage Act of 1792 set $1 at 371.25 grains of silver (0.77344 troy oz) and 24.75 grains of gold (0.05156 troy oz). In this bimetallic system, at a ratio of 15:1, a specific mass of gold and silver defined the dollar. Over the next 179 years, each passage of a new Coinage Act lightened the mass defining the dollar. To now, where no mass defines the dollar, but rather a paper decree of infinite supply. What used to be $1, is today in mass: - in silver - in gold
